Letter

Letter to the Editor

Dear Editor, As our country embraces the legalization of cannabis, we must remain vigilant against repeating the same public health mistakes we made with tobacco. Decades ago, tobacco industry leaders stood before Congress and falsely claimed their products were harmless. Today, the marijuana industry is doing the same—downplaying the risks of increasingly potent cannabis products while aggressively marketing to consumers. Research has shown clear links between high-potency cannabis and serious health outcomes. A study by Jouanjus et al. (2011) documented numerous emergency room visits tied to cannabis use, ranging from cardiovascular issues to acute psychiatric symptoms. Unlike the low-THC marijuana of the past, today’s products often exceed 20–30% THC, making them far more powerful and potentially harmful. The public deserves transparency and regulation. We failed to act quickly enough with cigarettes, and millions paid the price. Let’s not wait for another public health crisis to unfold. We must demand strict oversight of cannabis potency, clearer labeling, and honest dialogue about its risks.

Response to the Letter

Dear Editor, The concerns raised about high-potency cannabis are valid, particularly when viewed through the lens of public health history. Comparing the marijuana industry to Big Tobacco is a powerful reminder of what can happen when profit outweighs responsibility. However, the solution isn’t to instill fear—it’s to ensure that policy is guided by current science and public education. While the Jouanjus et al. (2011) study does highlight medical incidents related to cannabis, the broader context shows that such cases are relatively rare compared to other substances like alcohol or prescription opioids. Instead of prohibition-style thinking, we need modern regulatory strategies THC potency caps, clearer dosage standards, and consumer education campaigns that reflect both the risks and benefits. Marijuana legalization is an opportunity to do better than we did with tobacco. Let’s use this moment not to panic, but to build smart, proactive policies that prioritize health, safety, and informed choice.
