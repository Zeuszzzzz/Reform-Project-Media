Profile

The story of a woman in her 30s, as reported by Fraser (2019), illustrates the often-hidden risks of cannabis addiction despite legalization efforts. According to Fraser, this woman works for the government, is a mother, and is active in her community, yet she struggled with cannabis abuse that began in her teenage years. She described having a "highly addictive personality" and experiencing trauma from sexual assault, which led her to self-medicate with cannabis, hashish, and alcohol. Although she initially planned to attend university, her addiction led her to spend much of her early 20s partying, demonstrating how cannabis can derail personal and professional goals.

Fraser (2019) further details how the woman’s relapse occurred after a decade of abstinence when she tried cannabis socially, believing she was in control. Despite cannabis not being legal at the time, she accessed it easily through friends and growers. A prescription from her doctor for cannabis to manage back pain unintentionally reinforced her addiction, underscoring potential medical system oversights regarding patients with prior addiction histories. Her story challenges the notion that legalization or medical use inherently equates to safe or controlled consumption.

The article highlights how legalization gave the woman a socially acceptable justification to continue using cannabis, even as it negatively affected her work, finances, and family life. Fraser (2019) recounts a harrowing incident where she experienced a panic attack in public after accidentally ingesting too much cannabis oil, which became a turning point toward seeking help. Her guilt and the strain on her relationships reflect broader societal risks of normalizing cannabis use, especially when users downplay addiction by comparing it to other substances.

Fraser (2019) also includes expert commentary from Dr. Amanda Hudson, who confirms a 50% rise in cannabis-related addiction cases in Prince Edward Island since legalization. Dr. Hudson explains that cannabis use disorder, including withdrawal symptoms and cannabis-induced psychosis, is increasingly common, particularly among young adults and heavy users. This clinical perspective, paired with the woman’s personal experience, underscores the complex health challenges legalization may exacerbate.

By examining this woman’s struggle as detailed in Fraser (2019), it becomes clear that cannabis addiction can quietly devastate lives and families. Her story demonstrates the importance of cautious cannabis policies that recognize potential harms and prioritize prevention, treatment, and public education over unfettered legalization.
